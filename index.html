Wildfire History

Early Records and Observations: Historically, wildfires have always been a natural part of the ecosystems in the Western United States, particularly in areas dominated by forests and shrublands adapted to fire. Indigenous peoples used fire as a land management tool for thousands of years, influencing fire regimes in significant ways.

20th Century: With the establishment of modern fire suppression policies in the early 20th century, particularly after devastating events like the 1910 "Big Blowup" which burned about 3 million acres across Idaho, Montana, and Washington, the approach to wildfires shifted dramatically. The U.S. government began a policy of aggressive fire suppression, which reduced the number of fires in the short term but also led to an accumulation of undergrowth and dead wood, which are significant fuels for fires.

Late 20th Century to Present: From the 1980s onward, there has been a noticeable increase in the number and size of wildfires. Research indicates that this change is driven by a combination of factors including past fire suppression policies, increased human settlement in fire-prone areas (urban-wildland interface), and climate change impacts such as warmer temperatures, earlier snowmelt, and longer dry seasons.

Definition and Importance of WUI

The Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI) is defined as areas where human-made structures and infrastructure (homes, businesses, etc.) are in or adjacent to areas prone to wildland fire. This interface has become a focal point for wildfire management because it's where human populations and infrastructure are most at risk from wildfires.

Historical Context and Development



Early Development: In the early and mid-20th century, the expansion of urban areas into wildland areas was relatively limited. However, as population growth accelerated in the Western U.S., more communities began to develop in fire-prone landscapes, particularly in states like California, Colorado, and Arizona.

Late 20th Century: By the 1980s and 1990s, there was a significant increase in the development of the WUI. This growth was driven by a desire for scenic vistas, privacy, and a closeness to nature. However, this expansion led to increased vulnerability to wildfires as more people and property were situated in areas with a high fire risk.

Trends and Changes



Population Growth: The population in WUI regions has continued to grow rapidly. From 1990 to 2010, the number of new homes built in WUI areas was significantly higher than in non-WUI areas, underscoring the desirability of these regions despite their risks.

Increasing Wildfire Risks: As more people move into WUI areas, the risk of wildfires increases, not only because there are more ignition points (like vehicles, power lines, and residential activities) but also because fire suppression becomes harder. Wildfires that reach these populated areas are more challenging to manage and pose higher risks to human life and property.

Impact of WUI on Wildfire Management and Policy

The growth of WUI areas has significant implications for wildfire management policies and practices:



Fire Management Practices: here has been a shift towards more integrated fire management practices, including prescribed burns (controlled fires used to manage vegetation) and the creation of defensible spaces around properties.

Building and Zoning Regulations: Many states and local governments in the Western U.S. have updated building codes and zoning regulations to improve fire resistance in WUI areas. These include requiring fire-resistant building materials and creating regulations that require homeowners to manage vegetation around their properties.
